Cosmos Reason 2 — MTB Video Analysis

Can a Physics-Aware VLM Analyze Mountain Bike Footage Zero-Shot?
Travis Fraser • March 30, 2026 • UC Berkeley Haas School of Business

Executive Summary

NVIDIA's Cosmos Reason 2 is a vision-language model trained on first-person robotic and autonomous vehicle footage for physical AI applications. This experiment tested whether its spatial reasoning capabilities transfer to an entirely different domain: mountain bike helmet camera video.

The finding: Cosmos correctly identifies structural terrain features (trail surface, obstacles, difficulty, drop-off exposure) but confabulates environmental state (moisture, weather, elevation direction). The boundary maps precisely to its training distribution: spatial-geometric reasoning transfers; environmental-dynamic reasoning does not.

The 8B model eliminated most failures seen in the 2B (format errors, water hallucination, missed drop-offs) but produced more convincing hallucinations when it did confabulate, including fabricating observations physically impossible given the camera position.

For Strava specifically: This experiment validates that world foundation models can extract actionable terrain and risk data from crowdsourced rider footage zero-shot. With Strava's data assets (millions of GPS-tagged activity videos, segment metadata, rider telemetry), modest fine-tuning would likely close the confabulation gap. The Cosmos Cookbook explicitly covers post-training workflows for exactly this kind of domain adaptation.

Experiment Design

Hypothesis

Cosmos Reason 2 was trained on egocentric (first-person) video from robots and autonomous vehicles. Mountain bike helmet camera footage is structurally identical: egocentric perspective, forward-facing, navigating physical terrain. If the model's spatial reasoning is genuinely general, it should transfer. If it is overfit to industrial environments, it won't.

Prompt Design — Three Iterations

We developed three tiers of prompts, each informed by failures in the previous tier:

Tier 1: Open-Ended (7 categories)

Standard analytical prompts: terrain analysis, rider dynamics, trail conditions, segment narration, risk assessment, technical skills, and exploratory freeform. These gave the model maximum freedom to describe what it observed.

Problem discovered: The model filled every category comprehensively, hallucinating details when it couldn't determine them from the video. It invented puddles, water crossings, uphill sections, and "steep drops" that didn't exist, while missing the actual drop-offs beside the trail.

Tier 2: Constrained (4 categories)

Forced-choice answers (YES/NO, WET/DRY, category selection) with an explicit "CANNOT DETERMINE" escape hatch. Designed to shrink the hallucination surface area.

Result: Hallucination rate dropped approximately 50%. The model correctly answered "NO" to drops on the trail, identified obstacles as off-trail rather than on-trail, and stopped inventing water. However, it still hallucinated uphill sections and missed the lateral drop-offs.

Tier 3: Zone-Specific (5 categories)

Deterministic frame decomposition: each video frame is spatially cropped into five zones (left 30%, right 30%, center 40%, top 35%, bottom 30%). Zone-specific prompts analyze only what appears in the cropped region. This is preprocessing doing the work instead of prompt engineering.

Result (2B): The 2B model produced identical answers across all frames and both sides, suggesting it wasn't performing genuine per-frame visual grounding. This revealed a fundamental model capacity issue, not a prompt issue.

Result (8B): Peripheral exposure prompts correctly identified "rocky outcrop, VERY STEEP, DANGEROUS, SIGNIFICANT (10ft+)." The zone approach works at sufficient model scale.

Key Findings

1. Structural Spatial Reasoning Transfers

Both models correctly identified: singletrack trail, rocky/technical terrain, roots present, continuous descent, trail difficulty level. These are spatial-geometric features, the same class of observations the model makes about warehouse floors and road surfaces. The representation is general.

2. Environmental State is Confabulated

The 2B model invented water, puddles, mud, and cloud cover on a bone-dry, clear-sky day. The 8B eliminated the water hallucination but fabricated "dust kicked up behind the cyclist" as evidence of dryness. This is physically impossible from a forward-facing chest-mounted camera. The model reached the correct conclusion through fabricated evidence, a more dangerous failure mode than being obviously wrong.

3. The Uphill Hallucination Is Architectural

Both models intermittently claimed uphill sections on a continuously downhill trail. The likely cause: in autonomous vehicle training data, camera pitch angle correlates with road gradient. On an MTB trail, transitions from steep to less-steep sections change camera pitch without changing direction. The model's learned prior (pitch = gradient) produces false positives.

4. Drop-Off Detection Requires Scale + Zone Splitting

The 2B model never detected the steep left-side drop-off from full-frame video, even with direct prompting. The 8B detected drop-offs from full-frame video but claimed "both sides" when only the left side drops off (the right side is a dirt wall). The zone-splitting approach, which crops left and right peripheral strips for independent analysis, is the correct architectural solution, but requires 8B+ to work.

5. The 8B is a Better Hallucinator

The 2B's hallucinations are easy to catch: it invents water that obviously isn't there. The 8B's hallucinations are plausible and domain-appropriate: fabricated dust observations, "wooden bridges" and "jumps" that sound like real trail features. For any production use case, this means the 8B requires a stronger validation layer, not a weaker one.

6. Risk Assessment is the Strongest Category

Across both models and all prompt tiers, risk assessment produced the most useful and most accurate output. Trail difficulty, obstacle identification, escape route assessment, and exposure warnings are well-suited to the model's spatial reasoning strengths and least affected by environmental confabulation.

What Strava Has That This Experiment Doesn't

Data assets that change the equation:
  • Millions of GPS-tagged activity videos from Beacon and Clips, with rider consent for processing
  • Segment-level metadata: gradient profiles, surface type from community tags, difficulty ratings, popularity scores
  • Rider telemetry time-synced to video: speed, power, heart rate, cadence, with sub-second GPS alignment
  • The compute budget to fine-tune: The Cosmos Cookbook explicitly covers post-training workflows for domain adaptation. This is the exact use case
  • Ground truth at scale: millions of riders' GPS traces over the same segments provide statistical terrain ground truth that no single experiment can replicate

What This Experiment Proves That Matters

Validated capabilities:
  • A physics-aware VLM can extract terrain and dynamics information from helmet cam footage zero-shot, with no MTB-specific training
  • Risk assessment (exposure, obstacles, difficulty, sightlines) works well enough to be useful even without fine-tuning
  • The base spatial reasoning is there; it understands trail geometry, obstacle placement, and terrain structure from egocentric video
  • The confabulation problem is predictable: it maps to the training distribution boundary (spatial-static works, environmental-dynamic doesn't)
  • With even modest fine-tuning on labeled MTB data, the confabulation problem likely shrinks dramatically because the model already has the spatial representation; it just needs grounding in the right domain
  • Zone-based frame decomposition (deterministic preprocessing) outperforms prompt engineering for directing model attention to specific spatial regions

The Product Opportunity

Segment Risk Layer for Strava

Strava segments have GPS-derived difficulty ratings (gradient, distance) but no visual risk data. Rider footage analyzed by a physics-aware VLM could add a risk layer covering: exposure/drop-offs, blind corners, technical features, surface hazards, and escape routes. Crowdsourced footage from multiple riders on the same segment provides statistical robustness.

Acquisition Path

Strava's existing user base already records with GoPro, Insta360, and DJI Action cameras via Bluetooth sync. Prompted uploads on popular segments ("Help rate this segment") or integration with camera cloud pipelines would build the dataset. The footage doesn't need to be pristine; it needs to be helmet or chest cam at reasonable resolution.

Post-Training Path

The Cosmos Cookbook documents domain adaptation workflows. With Strava's labeled data (GPS-validated terrain profiles, community surface tags, rider speed/power traces), fine-tuning Cosmos on even a few thousand labeled MTB clips would ground the model's spatial reasoning in cycling-specific terrain. The representation already generalizes; it needs domain-specific calibration.

The pitch is not "I built a trail analysis product."
